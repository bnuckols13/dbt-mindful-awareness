What mindfulness actually is

Mindfulness is paying attention on purpose, in the present moment, without judgment. That's it. Three parts, all connected.

  • On purpose means you're choosing where your attention goes
  • Present moment means now, not yesterday or what comes next
  • Without judgment means noticing without labeling it good or bad
Mindfulness is not about clearing your mind. It's not a relaxation technique, though sometimes it does relax you. It's about noticing. That's the whole thing.
◆
Wise Mind

We have two ways of moving through the world. Emotion Mind is when feelings are driving everything. Reasonable Mind is when logic is running the show, often at the expense of what you actually feel.

  • Emotion Mind: reactive, intense, feelings feel like facts
  • Reasonable Mind: analytical, detached, can miss what matters
  • Wise Mind sits in the middle, holding both at once
Wise Mind often feels like a quiet knowing. It's not loud or desperate. You usually recognize it after the fact at first. With practice, you can get there in the moment.
◇
The What Skills: Observe, Describe, Participate

The What Skills tell you what to do when you practice mindfulness.

  • Observe — notice without words. Just watch what's happening inside and around you, without grabbing onto it or pushing it away
  • Describe — put words to what you observe. Stick to facts. "My chest feels tight" instead of "I'm having a panic attack and something is wrong"
  • Participate — fully enter what you're doing. No self-consciousness, no watching yourself from the outside. Just be in it
You use one at a time. Observing and describing are different things. Participating means you've put the clipboard down.
✦
The How Skills: Non-Judgmentally, One-Mindfully, Effectively

The How Skills describe the way you practice. They apply to anything.

  • Non-judgmentally — stick to facts, not evaluations. "I yelled" instead of "I'm a terrible person." This doesn't mean approval. It doesn't mean pretending things don't hurt
  • One-mindfully — one thing at a time. When you eat, eat. When you talk to someone, talk to them. Your attention goes where you are
  • Effectively — do what works, not what feels right or what's "fair." Effectiveness is about outcomes, not righteousness
These three can be practiced independently. You don't have to have all three going at once. Pick one and try it for a day.
◈
Non-judgment is not neutral

A common misconception: non-judgment means not caring, or not having opinions. That's not what it means.

  • Non-judgment means acknowledging what is, without adding a layer of good/bad/should/shouldn't
  • You can still have preferences. You can still feel pain. You can still want things to be different
  • The shift is in how you talk to yourself about it
The difference between "I made a mistake" and "I am a mistake." The first is a fact. The second is a judgment layered on top of a fact. Only one of them leaves room to move.

Discussion 20–25 min

These questions are meant to open things up, not have right answers. Let the conversation go where it needs to go.

Opening
What does your inner judgment voice sound like? What does it say most often?
The How Skills
Where in your life is it hardest to be non-judgmental? Toward yourself, toward others, or toward situations?
Wise Mind
Think of a time you knew something quietly, without it being loud or dramatic. What was that like?
Non-judgment in practice
What's the difference between letting yourself off the hook and being non-judgmental?
Let the group sit with this one. There's no quick answer.
Closing round
One moment this week you could practice observing without judging. What would that look like?
