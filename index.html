Mindfulness Core Skills

Mindfulness is not about emptying your mind. It's about noticing what's already there, without turning it into a verdict.

DBT's mindfulness skills are the foundation everything else builds on. They don't require you to be calm, spiritual, or good at sitting still. They just ask you to look at what's happening right now, as clearly as you can, without adding a layer of judgment on top of it.

Today we're focusing on two things: awareness (what you notice) and non-judgment (how you hold what you notice). Both are learnable. Both take practice. Neither requires perfection.

Opening Practice

5 min

Before we talk about mindfulness, let's do a small amount of it. We'll use the breath, not because it's special, but because it's always available.

breathe
One Minute Together

Find a position that feels okay for your body. You don't have to close your eyes. Notice the sensation of one breath: air coming in, a brief pause, air going out. When your attention wanders (and it will), just notice that it wandered and bring it back. No score-keeping. Just returning.

That returning? That's the practice. Not the stillness. The return.

Keep this brief — 60 to 90 seconds. After, ask: "What did you notice?" Don't push for deep observations. "My mind wandered" is a perfect answer. Affirm the noticing, not the stillness.

Three States of Mind

10 min

In DBT, we talk about three states of mind. Most of us spend time in all three. None of them is wrong. But one of them is where mindfulness is trying to take us.

Emotion Mind
Feeling Is Fact
Emotions drive thinking and behavior. "I feel it, so it must be true." Intense, reactive, sometimes overwhelming.
+
Wise Mind
Both, Together
The overlap of feeling and logic. Knows what is true and what matters. Quieter, but it's always there.
+
Reasonable Mind
Logic Is Everything
Analytical, cool, fact-focused. "Feelings are not relevant. Just look at the data." Can dismiss what's real.
What Wise Mind Feels Like

It tends to feel quieter than Emotion Mind and warmer than Reasonable Mind. Some people describe it as a sense of "settling." It's not always easy to find, but mindfulness is one of the main ways to get there.

Some people resist the idea that they have a Wise Mind at all, especially if they've been told their instincts are wrong. Affirm: "It may be harder to access for some of us, especially if our environment never made it safe to trust ourselves. That doesn't mean it isn't there."

The Mindfulness Skills

15 min

DBT breaks mindfulness into two sets: What skills (what you do when you practice) and How skills (how you do it). Together they describe a complete practice.

what skills
👁

Observe

Notice your experience without trying to change it or push it away. Watch thoughts like clouds passing through. Watch sensations without immediately reacting to them. "I notice tension in my chest." Not: "My chest is tight and something is wrong."
📝

Describe

Put words on what you observe, sticking to facts. Describe the experience, not your interpretation of it. This is harder than it sounds. "I'm having the thought that I'm not good enough." Not: "I'm not good enough."
🌊

Participate

Throw yourself fully into what you're doing. Stop standing outside your life and observing it from a distance. Participate without self-consciousness. Dancing, cooking, a conversation, a task, fully in it. Not watching yourself do it.
how skills
⚖️

Non-Judgmentally

Stick to the facts. Describe without evaluating. "Good," "bad," "should," "shouldn't" are judgments. Notice them when they show up and see if you can put them down. Not pretending things don't hurt. Describing what happened without adding a verdict.
🎯

One-Mindfully

Do one thing at a time. When you're eating, eat. When you're listening, listen. When your mind pulls elsewhere, gently return. This is the core gesture of mindfulness. Not multitasking. Not rehearsing tomorrow while living today.
🔧

Effectively

Do what works for the situation, not what feels right or fair. Let go of "I shouldn't have to" in favor of what actually moves things forward. Play the hand you have. Not giving up your values. Choosing behavior that serves your actual goals.
Quick Practice: Observe and Describe

Look at your hands for 30 seconds. Observe without judgment. Then, in your head or out loud, describe what you see sticking only to facts: color, texture, shape, any sensations. Notice when you slip into evaluation ("my hands look old," "my skin is bad") and see if you can swap it for description ("there are lines across the knuckles," "the skin is warm").

The goal is not to feel neutral about your hands. It's to notice the difference between describing and judging.

Non-Judgment: What It Is and Isn't

15 min

Non-judgment is one of the most misunderstood DBT skills. People often think it means pretending nothing is wrong, approving of everything, or becoming emotionally flat. None of that is true.

Non-Judgment Is NOT

  • Approving of things that hurt you
  • Pretending painful things don't hurt
  • Saying everything is fine when it isn't
  • Suppressing your feelings or opinions
  • Excusing behavior that caused harm

Non-Judgment IS

  • Describing what happened without adding a verdict
  • Separating the facts of a situation from your interpretation
  • Noticing judgment when it shows up, without judging yourself for judging
  • Releasing the layer of "should" that lives on top of pain and makes it heavier
  • Treating yourself with the same observational curiosity you'd offer a good friend
The Core Shift

Judgment doesn't just describe reality. It amplifies suffering. "I'm having a hard day" and "I'm a disaster who can't handle anything" describe the same morning, but they don't feel the same. The second one is a judgment chain, and it creates a whole second layer of pain on top of the first.

judgment vs. description
📋

Putting It into Words

The difference between a judgment and a description is concrete. Here's what it looks like in practice:

Judgment Description
"I'm a failure." "I didn't meet the deadline I set."
"I'm so stupid." "I made an error I wish I hadn't made."
"They're a terrible person." "They said something that hurt me."
"I should be over this by now." "I'm still feeling this. That's where I am."
"This is pointless." "I'm not seeing progress yet and that's frustrating."

Watch for the belief that non-judgment means weakness or self-excuse. A useful reframe: "Non-judgment actually takes more strength than judgment. Judgment is quick. Describing carefully and staying with what's real takes more work."

If someone shares a heavy self-judgment in the reflection, don't rush to fix or reframe it for them. Ask: "What would it look like to describe that without the verdict?" Let them find the language.

Group Discussion

20 min
◇ These are invitations, not obligations. You can pass any time.
  • Opening Round

    What state of mind do you find yourself in most often — Emotion Mind, Reasonable Mind, or somewhere in between? What does it feel like when you're there?

  • The Skills

    Which of the six skills (Observe, Describe, Participate, Non-Judgmentally, One-Mindfully, Effectively) feels most natural to you? Which one is the biggest stretch?

  • Non-Judgment

    Where is judgment hardest to put down in your own life? Toward yourself, toward others, or toward situations? What does the judgment sound like when it shows up?

  • The Returning

    During the opening practice, attention probably wandered. What was it like to notice that and come back? Was there judgment in the noticing?

  • Wise Mind

    Can you think of a time when you were in Wise Mind — when something felt settled or clear rather than urgent? What helped you get there?

If someone is self-judging heavily during the discussion (about their mindfulness practice, their mind wandering, their inability to stay present), gently name it: "Notice that what just happened there is exactly what we're practicing with — can we look at that judgment without adding more on top of it?"

Avoid praising people for being "good at mindfulness." The practice is not about performance.

Closing Practice

5 min

One more breath together before we close. This time, after you notice the breath, bring to mind one thing from today and just observe it. No verdict. No plan. Just notice that it's there.

notice

Keep this to 60 seconds. After: "One word for what you're leaving with today?" Go around. Short is fine. The word doesn't have to be positive.

Between Now and Next Time

Small Invitations: Choose What Fits

  • Pick one daily activity (brushing teeth, making coffee, walking) and do it one-mindfully for one week. When you wander, just return.
  • Notice three judgments this week. Write them down. Then rewrite each one as a description.
  • Check in with Wise Mind once before making a decision. Ask: "What does the part of me that is both feeling and thinking say?"
  • Use the Observe skill for one difficult emotion: watch it like weather rather than becoming it.

The mind wanders. You return. That's not failure. That's the whole practice.
